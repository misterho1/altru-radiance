## The single hard line (most important rule)

Utah Code 58-11a-302.18(1)(b): a master esthetician MAY NOT perform any
service in the scope "for the treatment of medical, physical, or mental
ailments."

The compliance problem is almost never the service — it is the OUTCOME
FRAMING. The licensed services below are all permitted. Describing any of
them as treating, healing, curing, fixing, relieving, or resolving a
condition, disease, injury, pain, or diagnosis is NOT permitted.

R156-11a-612 reinforces this: before chemical exfoliation, microneedling,
or microdermabrasion, the client must be told the procedure is "for
cosmetic and not medical purposes" (unless performed under healthcare-
practitioner supervision). "Cosmetic, not medical" is the safe framing
anchor for the whole site.

## Guiding principle — the lane is beautification

Altru Radiance's identity is holistic: beauty is more than skin, and the
owner researches and assesses the whole picture of what makes a person
beautiful before isolating any one area. This philosophy is fully in
scope and should come through on the site. The lane is BEAUTIFICATION
and the appearance of the skin and body. Every service description,
including fascia and holistic-approach copy, must serve that purpose.
Philosophy, approach, and the assessment of the whole person are always
in scope. Health, corrective, and therapeutic outcomes are never. When
in doubt, ask: does this sentence sell beauty/appearance, or does it
claim to fix a condition? Only the first belongs on the site.

## Body contouring — full detail

There is NO Utah definition of "body contouring" as a named procedure
(checked: statute and R156-11a-102 definitions — absent in both).

What IS defined is the device. Utah Medical Practice Act: a "cosmetic
medical device" means tissue-altering, energy-based devices that have the
potential for altering living tissue. "Ablative" means removing tissue;
"nonablative" devices alter without removing. Body contouring is the
nonablative use of such a device (e.g. radiofrequency, ultrasound,
cryolipolysis, controlled heat/cooling).

THE REAL TEST is the device, not the word:
- Uses a tissue-altering energy-based device -> cosmetic medical device
  procedure -> Tier 2, full 58-1-506 chain applies.
- Purely manual / hands-only (fascia work, manual lymphatic massage,
  manual sculpting, body wraps) -> NOT a cosmetic medical device
  procedure -> Tier 1, no 58-1-506 supervision.

If body contouring at Altru Radiance uses an energy device, 58-1-506(3)
requires ALL of the following:
1. A physician (unrestricted MD), nurse practitioner (unrestricted APRN),
   or physician assistant develops a treatment plan AND evaluates the
   patient in-person or by live telemedicine before treatment begins.
2. The supervising MD/APRN provides GENERAL cosmetic medical procedure
   supervision for a master esthetician performing the procedure.
   "General supervision" (58-1-506(1)(d)) = supervisor has authorized the
   procedure, is available in person in a timely manner to handle an
   adverse reaction, and is located within 60 minutes or 60 miles of the
   facility. The supervisor need NOT be in the room or building.
3. Posting/paperwork (58-1-506(4)): supervisor's name posted prominently;
   copy of supervisor's license on the wall; patient given written info
   naming + licensing both supervisor and performer; patient given a
   phone number answered within 24 hours; the supervising contract kept
   on premises.
4. Non-compliance is "unprofessional conduct" (58-1-506(5)).

Website implication: if Altru Radiance cannot satisfy this chain, body
contouring (and any energy-device service) should come OFF the site.
Manual, hands-on body treatments are unaffected and may stay.

## Fascia and the holistic-beauty philosophy

"Fascia" appears NOWHERE in the Utah master esthetics statute or rules.
It is therefore not a named authorized service. Whether fascia work is in
scope depends entirely on what the hands are physically doing — not on the
word. Scope cares about the activity, not the anatomy term.

The studio's identity is holistic: beauty is more than skin, and the
owner assesses the whole picture of what makes a person beautiful before
isolating any one area. This is a PHILOSOPHY, and philosophy is fully in
scope. Scope does not restrict how the owner thinks about beauty or how
comprehensively she assesses a client. It restricts the OUTCOME CLAIMS
attached to services. The whole fascia/holistic theme must therefore be
framed for ONE purpose only: beautification and the appearance of the
skin and body.

IN SCOPE — fascia framed as approach and beautification:
- Fascia-informed, holistic, whole-picture approach to beauty.
- Hands-on esthetic manipulation of the skin and surface tissue
  (covered by the statute's "manipulating ... using the hands" and
  lymphatic massage / body wrap language).
- Describing why the studio looks at the whole person, not one spot.
- Sensory and aesthetic experience of the work.

OUT OF SCOPE — fascia framed as therapy or correction:
- "Releases adhesions / restrictions," "corrects posture,"
  "relieves chronic pain," "resolves dysfunction," "heals injury."
- Any link between fascia work and a physical condition, pain, or
  diagnosis. The statute flatly bars services "for the treatment of
  medical, physical, or mental ailments." Fascia vocabulary in the
  bodywork world ("release," "restriction," "adhesion") is inherently
  therapeutic — these words may NOT appear as claimed results.

FLAG — the massage-therapy boundary (verify with DOPL):
Deep, structural manipulation of connective tissue also reads as massage
therapy, a SEPARATE Utah license with its own scope. Surface-level,
sculpting-style esthetic manipulation is master-esthetics work. Deep
structural bodywork may not be. The statutes do not draw this line
cleanly. If Altru Radiance's fascia work is deep/structural rather than
surface esthetic manipulation, STOP and ask Andrew to confirm scope with
DOPL (and whether separate massage credentialing applies). Do not assume.

Rule of thumb for all fascia/holistic copy: it must always read as
serving beautification and appearance. If a sentence implies a health,
corrective, or therapeutic outcome, it is out of lane — rewrite it as
approach/philosophy or remove the claim.

## Sauna

A sauna is NOT a cosmetic medical device — it applies ambient heat, it
does not direct energy to alter tissue. It does not trigger the 58-1-506
supervision chain. It sits in the wellness / hydrotherapy-adjacent zone.

FLAG for claims, not for supervision: sauna copy commonly attracts
medical claims ("detox," "reduce inflammation," "boost immunity,"
"treat"). These cross the medical-treatment hard line. Permitted:
relaxation, warmth, the sensory experience, beautification framing.

---

## Cupping

Manual cupping (suction only, no energy source) is NOT a cosmetic medical
device, so it does not trigger 58-1-506.

FLAG for scope, not for supervision: cupping is NOT named in the master
esthetics statute. It may be defensible as a hands/apparatus esthetic
procedure, but it also sits close to massage therapy, which is a separate
license. Whether a master esthetician may offer cupping at all is an open
scope question. STOP and ask Andrew to confirm with DOPL or his licensing
reference before cupping copy goes on the site. Outcome-framing rule also
applies (no "treats," "relieves," "detoxes").

## APPROVED phrasing patterns (scope-safe)

- "a cosmetic [service], not a medical treatment"
- "supports relaxation / a sense of wellbeing / the look and feel of skin"
- "a regenerative-inspired approach to skincare and the body"
- "advanced exfoliation," "advanced facials," "hands-on fascia work"
- describing the studio's philosophy, approach, and sensory experience

## FLAGGED phrasing patterns (avoid — implies medical treatment)

- treat, heal, cure, diagnose, relieve, remedy, fix, resolve
- "treats [condition]," any named medical/physical/mental condition
- detox / detoxify as a physiological claim
- "anti-aging" stated as a guaranteed result
- "medical-grade," "clinical results," "therapeutic" (as a health claim)
- pain, injury, disease, disorder, inflammation as something addressed
- biofield/quantum-biology language ONLY when it implies a HEALTH outcome
  (e.g., "biofield therapy treats X" or "biofield healing").

  Biofield itself is NOT banned. Mechelle uses "biofield" as practitioner
  language for how she palpates and observes — the body's perceived
  electromagnetic/energetic field — and that is in scope as either
  practitioner-observation framing OR philosophy framing.

  Approved patterns:
  - "biofield-informed observations" (assessment framing)
  - "Mechelle works with tissue, fascia, and biofield together" (description of
    her hands-on palpation method, not a health claim)
  - "Mechelle reads tissue, fascia, and biofield" (sensory practitioner verb)

  Flagged patterns (only these):
  - "biofield therapy heals/treats/cures X" (health-outcome claim)
  - "balances the biofield to resolve [condition]" (medical-outcome framing)
  - "biofield tuning" (sounds like a treatment service, not an observation)

  Biofield as Mechelle's first-person practitioner vocabulary is in scope.
  Biofield as a service or outcome claim is not.

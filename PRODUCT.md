## Register

brand

## Users

Three overlapping personas, all roughly 35-55, mostly women, Salt Lake County radius (Murray, SLC, Sandy, Holladay, Cottonwood Heights), 60-70% mobile traffic. Income tier supports a $250 signature service. Educated readers — comfortable with anatomical vocabulary if it's explained, not condescended to.

- **Skeptical-curious.** Has heard about fascia work, buccal release, or Mechelle's reputation. Wants proof it's real before booking. Job: verify Mechelle's credentials, see actual results, understand what physically happens in a session. Will read the Journal before clicking Book.
- **Injectable-fatigued.** Tried Botox or fillers, possibly multiple times. Came away wanting a different path — natural, structural, no needles, no downtime. Job: understand how this is genuinely different from injectables and whether results will be visible enough to justify the trade.
- **Somatic-wellness-native.** Already fluent in fascia, lymphatic, regulation language. Doesn't need explanation; needs signal that Mechelle is in the lane and operates at depth. Job: confirm sophistication, scan for credibility tells (anatomical specificity, Master Esthetician credential, mechanism honesty).

The site must address all three simultaneously through layered depth — a skim layer that converts skeptics, a contrast layer that reassures the fatigued, and a depth layer (Journal, anatomy glossary, philosophy framing) that satisfies natives. No persona is gated to a separate URL; they share every page.

## Product Purpose

Altru Radiance is a single-practitioner aesthetic wellness studio in Murray, Utah, owned and operated by Mechelle, a Licensed Master Esthetician. The studio's signature work is fascia-driven regenerative skincare — most distinctively the Restorative Buccal Facial, an intraoral fascia release rare in the Utah market.

The website's job is to convert qualified strangers into booked sessions while protecting the license. Success looks like:

1. A first-time visitor understands within 15 seconds that this is not a med spa, not injectables, not a relaxation day spa, and not wellness-woo.
2. The Restorative Buccal Facial is the clearest entry point and accounts for the majority of new-client bookings.
3. The Welcome Bundle and Foundation Package convert curious skeptics into multi-session commitments.
4. Every page passes a Utah DOPL scope-of-practice read: beautification framing only, never medical treatment claims.
5. The Journal builds long-tail SEO authority on fascia / buccal / regenerative skincare terms — and serves as the proof layer for skeptics.

## Brand Personality

Three-word personality: **clinical, editorial, unhurried.**

- **Clinical** — not in the cold lab-coat sense, but in the precise-vocabulary sense. Anatomy named directly: buccinator, masseter, pterygoids, SMAS, ECM. Mechanisms explained: mechanotransduction, piezoelectric effect, lymphatic flow. The voice respects readers as intelligent adults.
- **Editorial** — long-form, scene-setting, italicized ledes in Cormorant Garamond. Closer to a print magazine essay than a service brochure. The brand reads, it doesn't shout.
- **Unhurried** — no urgency tactics, no "Book Now Before Spots Fill Up." The pacing of the copy mirrors the pacing of the practice: deliberate, slow-resolving, depth over rush.

Voice anchors:
- Authoritative but warm — Mechelle is the practitioner speaking, not a brand committee.
- Honest about scope — call out marketing hype, refuse to overclaim, name what this is not.
- Mechanically minded — every claim runs through anatomy and physics, never "energy" or "vibration" or "glow from within."
- First-person from Mechelle for hands-on work ("I work the buccinator with..."); first-person plural reserved for studio philosophy.
- No emoji. No exclamation points. No em dashes (use commas, colons, semicolons, parentheses).

Emotional goals on the page:
- **Confidence without pressure.** Reader feels: she knows what she's doing, and she's not selling me.
- **Expertise without coldness.** Reader feels: anatomical depth, but the human is still here.
- **Depth without mysticism.** Reader feels: this is real, not vibes.

## Anti-references

Hard anti-reference (the primary trap to avoid):

- **Standard med-spa template.** Stock-photo smiling women with arms crossed, before/after slider as the hero, baby-pink and chrome metallic accents, "Specials This Month" banners, "Schedule Your Consultation" formality, generic "natural radiance" wordmark, navigation that leads with Botox and Fillers. The single biggest reflex any reviewer might have for a Utah aesthetics studio — and the exact thing this brand is the opposite of. If a stranger ever mistakes this site for a med spa, the design has failed.

Secondary anti-references:

- **Wellness-mystic.** Crystals, soft-focus everything, sage with hand-lettered fonts, "glow from within," Pinterest-mood-board energy. A live risk because fascia and holistic language can pull this direction if the visuals slip.
- **Corporate-clinical dermatologist.** Stark white plus navy, lab-coat photography, sterile geometry, "Schedule Your Consultation" formality, stock cross-section diagrams. A live risk because anatomical authority can overcorrect toward sterile-coldness.
- **Templated Squarespace day-spa.** Centered-everything beige minimalism, lavender field stock photos, "Mind Body Soul" headers, "Pamper Yourself" CTAs. The lazy default any wellness site falls into when no register has been chosen.
- **Procedure-marketplace heroes.** "Buccal Release — $250 — Book Today" formatted like a Groupon listing. Pricing belongs as supporting detail, never as headline.

Visual reflex-reject patterns (per impeccable shared design laws):

- Side-stripe accent borders on cards or sections.
- Gradient text effects.
- Decorative glassmorphism on cards.
- The hero-metric-tile-with-gradient SaaS template.
- Identical icon-plus-heading-plus-text card grids that repeat without variation.

## Design Principles

Five strategic principles. These shape every shape/craft/critique session going forward.

1. **Anatomy is the proof.** Specificity earns trust where claims can't. Name the buccinator, not "the cheek." Explain mechanotransduction, not "energy work." If a sentence could appear on a wellness Pinterest board, rewrite it with anatomy until it can't.

2. **Mechelle speaks, not a brand.** The practitioner is the product. First-person voice for all hands-on work. The Master Esthetician credential is the credential — never "our team," never "our experts." If the copy could come from a marketing department, it's wrong.

3. **Beautification, never treatment.** SCOPE.md (Utah Code 58-11a-302.18(1)(b)) is upstream design constraint, not downstream copy edit. Every claim sells appearance; no claim fixes a condition. Defensive disclaimers ("I don't diagnose airway conditions — see a dentist") are protective design features, not friction.

4. **Editorial over commercial.** Read Frieze, not Groupon. Italic Cormorant Garamond ledes set scene before service. Pricing and duration as supporting details, never as headline. Restraint signals confidence — the work doesn't need to shout.

5. **Three readers, one page.** The skeptic, the injectable-fatigued, and the somatic-native must all feel addressed on the same surface. Layered depth: skim layer of trust pills and named services for skeptics; contrast framing ("no needles, no machines, no downtime") for the fatigued; Journal depth, glossary terms, and philosophy framing for natives. No persona gets a dedicated URL — they share every page.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

**Target: WCAG 2.2 AA** across all pages, with particular care for accommodations relevant to the 35-55 demographic and 60-70% mobile traffic split.

Required behavior:

- **Color contrast.** Body text on `--black` background meets 4.5:1 minimum; large text and headings meet 3:1. `--mist` (`#8aaa8a`) labels on `--black` already pass; verify before adding any low-contrast secondary text.
- **Keyboard navigation.** All interactive elements reachable by Tab. Focus state visible (no `outline: none` without replacement). Skip-to-main-content link on every page.
- **Touch targets.** Minimum 24×24 CSS pixels (2.2 requirement). The current `nav-hamburger` at 44×44 and `hero-cta` at min-height 48 satisfy. Audit smaller pills and inline links going forward.
- **Motion.** Hero video stack cycles crossfades. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` — disable autoplay or pause on first frame. Same for any future scroll-driven motion.
- **Alt text.** Format already established: `[service/technique]-[descriptor]-[location]-altru-radiance`. Decorative-only images use empty `alt=""`.
- **Screen readers.** Live Google Reviews widget already uses `aria-current`, `aria-hidden`, `role="region"`, `aria-roledescription="carousel"`. Extend the same care to any new carousel or accordion.
- **Forms.** Square iframe is third-party; we can't fix its internal a11y. The `/book` intermediate page should be fully keyboard-navigable up to the iframe handoff.

Demographic-specific care:

- **35-55 eyesight.** Default body font size at the upper end of comfortable (current 0.9–0.95rem on body copy reads OK but verify). Avoid grey-on-grey or thin weights below 300 for body. Increase line-height where possible.
- **Vestibular sensitivity.** The cycling hero video stack with 1500ms crossfades is on the edge of safe — confirm `prefers-reduced-motion` actually halts it. Same audit for any future scroll-driven motion.
- **Cognitive load.** Layered depth (three-reader principle) demands clear visual hierarchy at the skim layer. Don't bury entry points; the skeptic must find Book within 15 seconds without reading anatomy.

What we explicitly do NOT need to support:

- Spanish-language localization (Murray is mostly English-speaking client base; not a current priority).
- Right-to-left layouts.
- Print stylesheets.

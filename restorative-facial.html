At a Glance

What is buccal massage?

Buccal massage is a manual facial technique in which the practitioner wears sterile gloves and works both outside and inside the cheek to manipulate the deep muscles, fascia, and connective tissue of the face. The word "buccal" refers to the cheek, from the Latin bucca. Unlike a standard facial that works only on the skin's surface, buccal massage accesses the interior surface of the cheek to reach the buccinator, masseter, and pterygoid muscles, which cannot be fully addressed from the outside.

At Altru Radiance in Murray, Utah, buccal massage is delivered as the central component of a 110-minute Restorative Facial, integrated with manual lymphatic drainage, structural facial work, and clinical skincare.

Why It Works

Where conventional skincare can't reach

Most facials operate on the skin's surface: cleansing, exfoliating, infusing serums. These have value, but they have a ceiling. The structural causes of facial heaviness, asymmetry, and the appearance of aging live beneath the skin, in the fascial network and the chronically contracted muscles of the jaw and midface.

Three layers, one access point.

Buccal massage releases fascial adhesions in the midface, resets masseter and pterygoid tension through intraoral access, and reactivates cervical and facial lymphatic flow. The result is structural change that no topical product can deliver: visible lift, jaw softening, and a face that sits the way it's meant to sit.

How is buccal massage different from a regular facial?

A regular facial works on the skin's surface: cleansing, exfoliating, applying actives. Buccal massage works on the structural layer beneath. Sterile gloves allow the practitioner to manipulate facial muscles and fascia from inside the cheek, accessing the buccinator, masseter, and pterygoid muscles that cannot be reached externally. The goal is structural lift and tension release, not surface optimization.

For a deeper comparison, see our article on myofascial release facial versus traditional facial.

Does buccal massage hurt?

Buccal massage involves deliberate pressure on areas of held tension, which most clients describe as a profound sense of release rather than pain. The intraoral portion begins with light pressure and builds gradually as tissue softens. Areas of chronic masseter or pterygoid tension may feel tender, but the work is never meant to be painful. Mechelle adjusts pressure throughout based on your feedback.

Is buccal massage safe?

Buccal massage performed by a trained, licensed esthetician using sterile gloves is a safe manual bodywork modality. Contraindications include active oral infections, cold sores, recent oral surgery, or being within two weeks of injectable treatments. Mechelle reviews your health history at intake and adjusts the session accordingly.

Can buccal massage help TMJ pain?

Yes. The medial pterygoid muscle is one of the primary contributors to TMJ dysfunction and is only fully accessible from inside the mouth. Buccal massage directly addresses this muscle along with the masseter and lateral pterygoid. Clients with chronic jaw clenching, bruxism, or referred ear and headache pain often experience meaningful relief after a series of buccal massage sessions. For more, read our article on TMJ relief through facial massage.

Is buccal massage a natural alternative to Botox?

Buccal massage and Botox work through different mechanisms. Botox blocks muscle contraction; buccal massage releases muscle tension and fascial restriction. For jaw and masseter concerns, buccal massage can achieve similar functional outcomes (jaw softening, reduced bulk) through a different pathway, with no injections, no recovery time, and no ongoing chemical exposure. See our journal article on natural alternatives to Botox for the full comparison.

How long do buccal massage results last?

Visible lift and decongestion from a single session typically remain noticeable for one to two weeks. Structural change builds cumulatively across a series and is sustained through monthly maintenance. Buccal massage does not create permanent change: it releases held tension and supports the body's natural lift, which the muscles and fascia will revert toward without ongoing care.

What should I do to prepare for my first buccal massage?

Avoid a heavy meal in the 1 to 2 hours before your session. Arrive with a clean face. Disclose any TMJ symptoms, recent dental work, or recent injectables (wait at least two weeks after Botox, Dysport, or filler). Drink water before and after to support the lymphatic component of the work.
